# Social media strategy

## Who we're talking to

The core audience is people who get frustrated that public data exists but nobody presents it clearly. Think: the person who listens to More or Less on Radio 4. Who reads Tim Harford. Who knows the ONS website exists but finds it unusable. Who argues in the pub using numbers and then has to say "I think I read that somewhere."

They're not activists. They're not doom-scrollers looking for confirmation that everything is terrible. They're curious, slightly data-literate adults who feel like they're being talked down to by vague news coverage and up-sold to by partisan think tanks.

They want to feel *informed*, not *outraged*.

They share things that make them look smart, not angry.

The ideal response to a WIAH post is: "Huh. I didn't know that. Sending this to Dave."

---

## The emotional register

**Not:** "The government has DESTROYED the NHS."
**Yes:** "GP wait times have doubled since 2015. Here's what the data actually shows."

**Not:** "Things are getting worse and worse."
**Yes:** "This surprised me: beach water quality is actually better than it was in the 1990s. But something else has got much worse."

**Not:** "Share this if you're angry."
**Yes:** "If you want to look this up yourself, it's all on the ONS website. We've just made it readable."

The tone is: *I found this interesting and I thought you might too.* Not: *you should be furious about this.*

It's Radio 4, not Twitter outrage. It's QI, not a campaign leaflet.

## Platforms

**Twitter / X**
Primary. This audience is still there. Short posts work; threads work even better. Stat → context → link. The format of "I spent an afternoon in the data and here's what I found" performs well.

**LinkedIn**
Underrated for this. Professionals who care about public services, policy, data. Posts here can be slightly longer and more considered. "The thing nobody puts in the headline" framing works well. People share things on LinkedIn that they wouldn't share on Twitter because it feels less political.

**Bluesky**
Growing and exactly the right demographic: people who left Twitter for a calmer, smarter feed. Lean in.

**Instagram**
Chart cards only. Clean, high-contrast. Not a primary channel but the OG-image format works as a static post.

## Content formats

### The single stat
One number. Two lines of context. A link.
Best for striking figures that don't need explanation.
Keep to 3 lines max.

### The counter-narrative
"You probably think X. The data says Y."
Best for surprising findings — especially positive ones.
The positive surprises are the most shareable. People want to pass on something that complicates the simple story.

### The comparison
"Then vs now." "Best vs worst." "Your area vs the national average."
Highly shareable because it's specific and personal.
The local angle ("your area") is gold.

### The thread
5–8 posts. One topic. Build it like a story.
Open with the most surprising finding. Then show the data. Then the context. Then the link.
End with: "All of this is public data. We just made it readable."
Threads work because this audience will read them. They're not skimming — they're interested.

### The "I looked this up" post
Conversational. First person (brand voice, not personal).
"We spent the afternoon in the ONS Housing data. Five things that surprised us."
Positions the site as the person who does the digging you don't have time to do.

## What makes something shareable for this audience

1. **It surprises them.** Including positively. Especially positively.
2. **It's specific.** A number, a year, a place. Not "much worse."
3. **It's their area.** "Enter your postcode" is the most powerful hook we have.
4. **It respects their intelligence.** No outrage bait. No "you won't believe this."
5. **It shows the working.** Linking to source data is not a footnote — it's the product. It's what separates us from a think tank.
6. **It's not partisan.** The data is the data. This is what makes it shareable across the political spectrum.

---

## What to avoid

- Outrage framing ("they've destroyed", "nobody is doing anything")
- Vague language ("significantly worse", "a crisis")
- Campaigning language ("we need to", "demand better")
- Posting the same type of thing every time — mix it up
- Being relentlessly gloomy — the counterintuitive positive findings are essential
- Long hashtag lists — one topical hashtag maximum, often none

## The postcode hook

Every topic page has a postcode lookup. This is the most powerful social tool we have and it's underused.

Posts that reference the lookup perform better:
- "We found the ICB with the longest GP wait times. Yours might be different. [link]"
- "National averages hide enormous regional variation. Enter your postcode to see where your area sits."

The lookup makes the data personal. Personal data gets shared.

---

## Frequency and cadence

- 3–4 posts per week per platform
- Mix topics — don't run three water posts in a row
- One thread per week, rotating topic
- When new data drops (NHS monthly stats, ONS releases) — post same day

## The 50 posts

The 50 posts in `posts.md` are the content bank. They work as written but should be treated as raw material — many benefit from a more conversational opener or a "counter-narrative" reframe before publishing. The ones that work best as-is are the comparison and counter-narrative posts (the "surprising" ones). The straight stat posts benefit from a hook line before the data.
