**2.** [COUNTER]
Here's something the NHS coverage usually misses.

The health service handles around 1.4 million GP appointments every working day.

The system isn't broken. But a specific part of it — access, getting through the door — has got measurably and consistently worse over a decade.

Those are two different stories.

**3.** [COUNTER]
Assumption: the areas with the fewest GPs have the longest waits.

Reality: not always. Some of the worst-performing ICBs have reasonable GP numbers. The constraint isn't always headcount — it's patient demand growing faster than any recruitment can match.

The data is more complicated than the simple story.

## HOUSING

**11.** [THREAD OPENER]
In 1997: the average house cost about 3.5× the average annual salary.
Now: closer to 9×.

We pulled together 25 years of ONS and Land Registry data to understand how that happened.

Thread →
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/housing

**12.** [COUNTER]
The "avocado toast" theory of why people can't buy houses doesn't survive contact with the data.

The average first-time buyer age has risen from 29 to around 34 since the late 1990s. That's five more years of saving a deposit that keeps getting further away.

The arithmetic changed. The behaviour didn't.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/housing

---

**13.** [COUNTER]
Not everywhere is unaffordable.

Parts of the North East still have house prices below 4× local earnings. The national average conceals what are genuinely two different housing markets in the same country.

The aggregate figure does a lot of work hiding regional variation.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/housing

---

**14.** [COUNTER]
London's house price to earnings ratio peaked above 12× in 2016.

It's actually come down a little since then. It's still 10×. But the direction is worth knowing, because it complicates the simple story.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/housing

**15.**
Private rent as a share of median income. Every English region. Over the last decade.

Every single one went up.

Not a London story. Not a city story. A national one.

**16.**
The social housing waiting list is the longest it's been since records began.

New social homes built annually: near a 50-year low.

**17.** [COUNTER]

**18.**
Real-terms house price growth in England since 2000: roughly 170%.
Real-terms wage growth in the same period: roughly 11%.

Neither of those numbers is disputed. That's the whole situation.

**19.** [COUNTER]
First-time buyer numbers actually rose between 2013 and 2019. By quite a lot.

Help to Buy moved the dial. The argument is about whether it moved it towards the right fix or just brought forward demand that made prices worse.

The data shows the trend. The interpretation is still contested.

## WATER

England's water companies discharged sewage for over 3.6 million hours in 2023.

But the variation between companies is enormous — and almost nobody talks about it.

**22.** [COUNTER]
This one surprised us.

Bathing water quality at English beaches is significantly better than it was in the early 1990s. The long-run trend is genuine improvement.

The recent deterioration is also real. Both are true. The timeline matters.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

**23.**
One water company discharged sewage for roughly 10× more hours than another in 2023.

Same regulator. Same legal framework. Same country.

The variation is almost entirely a company-by-company story.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

**24.**
Rivers in "good ecological status" in England: under 16%.

That's not a pressure group's number. It's the Environment Agency's own classification. Their methodology. Their data.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

**25.**
Water company dividends since privatisation: tens of billions.
Water company debt accumulated since privatisation: also tens of billions.
Infrastructure investment: the third, smaller, number.

**26.** [COUNTER]
Something that's actually got better: the number of pollution incidents classed as "serious" has fallen significantly over the long term.

Something that's got worse: the number of lower-level continuous spills, measured in hours, has gone the other way.

The problem has changed shape. It hasn't gone away.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

**27.**
We ranked water companies by sewage discharge hours and by average bills.

The correlation between worst performer and highest bills isn't perfect. But it's interesting enough to look at.

**28.** [COUNTER]
Sewage spills spike after heavy rain. That's the design: combined storm overflows were built to release during exceptional events.

What changed: extreme rainfall events have become more frequent since 2000. The infrastructure wasn't designed for the climate it's operating in now.

This is an engineering problem as much as a regulation one.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

---

**29.** [COUNTER]
Scotland's rivers are in substantially better ecological health than England's.

Different regulatory model. Different investment structure. Similar rainfall. Same island.

The difference isn't natural. It's institutional.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/water

**30.**

The target is still published. The deadline passed. The number isn't zero.

**32.** [COUNTER]
This is the most counterintuitive thing in all five of our topics.

Overall crime in England and Wales — as measured by the Crime Survey, which tracks what actually happened to people — is substantially lower than it was in 1995. Much lower.

That's not a political point. It's what the survey data shows.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/justice

**33.**
Crown Court backlog: over 70,000 cases.

Some defendants are waiting more than three years from charge to trial.

Victims wait the same amount of time. That's rarely mentioned.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/justice

---

**34.**
Fraud is now the most common crime in England and Wales by volume.

The charge rate for fraud: around 1%.

Most of it is never reported to police. Most of what is reported isn't investigated. Most of what's investigated doesn't lead to a charge. That's not an opinion — it's the published outcomes data.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/justice

---

**35.**
Offences recorded by police since 2015: roughly stable.
Offences leading to a charge or summons: roughly halved.

The recording end of the system has improved. The outcome end has gone the other way.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/justice

---

**36.** [COUNTER]
Domestic abuse charge and conviction rates have risen significantly over the past decade.

Specialist courts. Better support for victims through the process. Different approach to evidence.

This is what targeted investment in a specific part of the system looks like in the data.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/justice

**38.**
Average time from offence to completion at Crown Court for some offence types: over 700 days.

Nearly two years. For everyone involved.

The backlog isn't an abstract statistic. It's a specific wait for a specific person.

**39.** [COUNTER]
Police recorded crime rose sharply after 2014 — which looks alarming until you read the context.

The HMIC had flagged systematic under-recording in the years before. The spike is partly a correction to previously incomplete data.

The numbers changed. Some of the reality had already happened.

**40.**
Knife crime prosecutions take roughly twice as long to reach court as they did a decade ago.

Detection rates haven't changed much. The constraint has moved downstream — from investigation to the courts.

The bottleneck shifted. The headline rarely reflects that.

**42.** [COUNTER]
The attainment gap between children on free school meals and their peers at GCSE has narrowed since 2011.

It's still 1.5 grades per subject.

**43.**
Teacher vacancy rates: tripled since 2015.

Hardest hit: maths, physics, computing.

The subjects with the highest vacancy rates happen to be the ones with the strongest private sector salary alternatives. That's not a coincidence.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/education

---

**44.** [COUNTER]
Something that's genuinely improved: the proportion of pupils getting grade 4 or above in English and maths at GCSE has risen substantially since 2017.

Setting a floor standard and measuring against it moved the dial. That's worth knowing alongside the things that haven't improved.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/education

---

**45.**
The statutory deadline for an Education, Health and Care Plan assessment: 20 weeks.

The median wait: now longer than that.

The number of children in the queue has risen every year since 2015.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/education

---

**46.**
Persistent absence — missing 10% or more of school days — affected around 1 in 10 pupils before the pandemic.

It now affects around 1 in 4.

The scale of that change suggests something more structural than post-Covid catch-up.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/education

---

**47.** [COUNTER]
England's PISA ranking in reading improved between 2018 and 2022.

That's the international comparison. England rose.

Worth putting alongside the domestic picture, which is more mixed.
whatisactuallyhappening.uk/education

**48.**
The attainment gap between the most and least deprived local authorities at age 11 is larger than the gap at age 5.

The gap between starting school and secondary school: widened, not closed.

Early career teacher retention has improved slightly over the last two years, after a sustained decline.

Pay rises moved the number. Not dramatically. But the direction changed.

The vacancy data hasn't caught up yet — but it's worth tracking.
